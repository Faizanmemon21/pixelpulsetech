WEBSITE ANALYSIS — ACTIVE THEORY (activetheory.net)
Source: Official site metadata, co-founder interview (Communication Arts, Feb 2024),
design-press coverage (DesignRush, Ad World Masters), company records (LeadIQ/ZoomInfo)
Note: Site is a fully JS/WebGL-rendered SPA — content below is compiled from the
studio's own published statements and verified third-party analysis rather than a
static HTML scrape.

4. BRAND VOICE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Confident, craft-obsessed, technical-but-passionate. Talks like makers, not
  marketers.
- Short, punchy, capability-forward sentences — leans on fragments for impact.
  Example (social copy): "Real-time 3D. WebGL. Shaders. On the web. At scale."
- Frequently pairs a technical noun with an emotional one: "story, art &
  technology," "passionate makers," "buttery-smooth UI."
- Understated about achievements — lets client logos (Google, Spotify, Vans,
  Burberry) and awards (Awwwards, FWA, Emmy) speak for themselves rather than
  over-claiming.
- First-person plural throughout ("we," "our") — collective studio voice, no
  single named spokesperson pushed forward on the site itself.
- In interviews, co-founders describe decisions in plain, unpretentious terms
  — e.g. on why they picked a simple nav: "We wanted something simple — a
  link to our work and how to contact us."


5. COLOUR
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Base theme colour (meta theme-color): #000000 — full black canvas.
- Predominantly dark/black background used as negative space, consistent
  across at least two full site redesigns (2017 "V-prior" version and 2024
  "V6").
- Light is used as the accent, not colour blocks: gradient light effects
  rising from the bottom of the screen, fading black-to-gray.
- Colour appears via accent/interactive elements rather than brand palette
  blocks: the multiplayer "tube" trails that spawn from cursor/touch are
  rendered in saturated, varied colours against the black field — colour is
  earned through interaction, not decoration.
- Overall palette reads as: black canvas + white/gray typography + dynamic
  saturated accent colour generated by user interaction (shader-driven, not
  static hex values).


6. NAVIGATION MENU
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Current (V6, live since Jan 2024):
- Deliberately minimal — two destinations only: Work and Contact.
- Rendered as a "pillbox" nav element that reacts dynamically to scroll
  velocity (speeds up / animates in response to how fast the user scrolls).
- Co-founder quote: "We wanted something simple — a link to our work and how
  to contact us. We went with a pillbox that reacts with scroll velocity."

Previous version (2017-era, for historical/reference contrast):
- Hamburger icon, top-left corner, present on every page.
- Opens a full-screen slide-over/overlay panel.
- A large, centrally-placed 3D-rendered version of the logo sits in the
  overlay, built with shaded/gradient materials so it appears to pop off the
  screen.
- Page links listed to the left of the logo, flat design, sans-serif type.
- Subtle parallax: logo and links shift slightly as the cursor moves across
  the screen.


7. HERO SECTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Current (V6):
- No traditional headline/subhead/CTA hero stack — the "hero" IS the
  interactive WebGL canvas itself, populated by colour-trail particles that
  respond to the user's mouse or touch.
- These trails are networked in real time: multiple visitors on the site
  simultaneously can see each other's cursor trails, creating a shared,
  ambient sense of "who else is here right now."
- An AI chat layer floats within this space, acting as the entry point to
  the studio's work rather than a static "Explore our work" button.

Previous version (2017-era):
- Hero built around a fractured/shattered-glass shader effect that follows
  the cursor as it moves across the screen — an immediate, tactile "this
  site is alive" first impression before any text is read.


8. SHADERS / BACKGROUND
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Core rendering technology: WebGL via JavaScript (confirmed directly by the
  studio's interactive director).
- Internally, the team has moved away from object-oriented code toward a
  state-based, functionally-programmed JavaScript architecture within their
  own framework — chosen specifically to make experiences like this faster
  to build.
- Named background/effect systems referenced by the studio:
  - Fractured glass shader (2017-era hero, cursor-reactive)
  - Networked "tube" light-trail shader (V6, spawns from mouse/touch at top
    and bottom of the viewport, synced across live visitors via
    multiplayer/networked state)
  - 3D logo render with gradient/shaded lighting rising from a black
    negative-space background (menu overlay, 2017-era)
- The studio has historically built custom in-house 3D tooling to support
  this level of shader/rendering work at scale, including:
  - "Hydra" — an in-house 3D engine optimized for maximum graphics
    throughput with reduced CPU usage
  - "Aura" — a cross-platform WebGL project spanning iOS, Android, Desktop,
    Desktop VR (Vive/Oculus), Mobile VR (Daydream/Oculus Go), and Magic Leap


9. SCROLL AREA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Scroll velocity is treated as a first-class input signal, not just a
  vertical-position tracker — it directly drives the animation state of the
  navigation pillbox.
- In the prior site version, scrolling top-to-bottom was the primary way to
  browse sequentially through case studies (as an alternative to the
  slide-out project sidebar).
- In V6, browsing is led conversationally by the AI chat layer as much as by
  scroll — the site blends "scroll to explore" with "ask to be shown"
  navigation patterns.


10. FEATURES SECTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Active Theory is an agency/studio site, not a SaaS product — so there is no
traditional bullet-point "features" grid. Its equivalent is a capability /
technology showcase, communicated through:
- Named proprietary tools (Hydra 3D Engine, Aura cross-platform WebGL) used
  as proof of technical depth rather than listed as abstract "features."
- Case-study depth over feature lists: each project functions as a proof
  point for a specific capability (real-time 3D, networked multiplayer
  state, shader work, large-scale live events).
- Ongoing public technical writing (Active Theory's Medium publication,
  "Active Theory Case Studies") which deep-dives into the technology behind
  specific projects — serving the same trust-building purpose a features
  page would on a product site.


11. PRICE / ACTION (pricing section)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Not applicable. As a bespoke creative/production studio, Active Theory does
not publish pricing, tiers, or packages anywhere in its public presence.
Engagement is inquiry-based via direct contact; third-party agency
directories list "Minimum budget: N/A" and "Hourly rate: —" for the studio.


12. CALL TO ACTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Primary CTA is implicit rather than a labelled button: the nav itself
  reduces to two actions — view Work, or Contact.
- Secondary/ongoing CTA: recruiting. The studio actively runs "We're
  hiring!" callouts on social channels pointing to careers.activetheory.net
  for design/dev roles (e.g. "Junior Designer," "Brand & Social Content
  Designer"), framed informally rather than as a corporate careers page.
- No sales-style CTAs ("Book a demo," "Start free trial," etc.) anywhere in
  the studio's public materials — consistent with a project-inquiry model
  rather than a product funnel.


13. CARDS (case-study / project cards)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Each project is represented by a single full-bleed image occupying the
  entire viewport — the image alone carries the project's mood/identity.
- Project name is placed in the bottom-left corner, set in sans-serif type.
- A short paragraph of supporting context accompanies the name — kept
  deliberately brief; the studio prompts visitors to click through to the
  live project itself rather than over-explaining on the card.
- Prior version: a slide-out sidebar from the right lists all projects with
  clickable tags, letting visitors filter by project type before committing
  to a full case study.
- Known project cards referenced across sources include: Spotify Wrapped /
  Wrapped Party, Google I/O ("Bubbles I/O 2017," "Paper Planes" via Google
  Creative Lab experiments), Wizarding World ("Discover Your Patronus,"
  "Welcome to Hogwarts" for Pottermore), US Air Force ("Airman Challenge"),
  IBM TechXchange real-time 3D data visualization, and The Wall Street
  Journal's "The Field" (an interactive galaxy/networking space built for
  the WSJ Future of Everything Festival).
